Astrophysics Black Holes

Supermassive black holes (SMBHs) are typically defined as black holes with masses above ~105 solar masses and below ~1010 solar masses. They are among the densest and most powerful objects in the universe, and observations suggest that nearly every large galaxy hosts a central SMBH.

How Do SMBHs Form?

Several pathways are proposed:

  • Growth from stellar-mass seeds: core-collapse supernovae form black holes that accrete gas and merge over billions of years to reach SMBH scales.
  • Direct collapse: massive, pristine gas clouds in the early universe may collapse directly into black holes (bypassing normal star formation), explaining very early SMBHs.
  • Mergers: repeated black-hole mergers (as seen by LIGO for stellar-mass BHs) may help build up to intermediate and supermassive scales.

Why SMBHs Matter

SMBHs shape galaxy evolution: they regulate star formation, drive energetic outflows, and influence the growth and structure of galaxies. Understanding them helps explain how the universe built its largest structures.
